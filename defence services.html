Key Notes on India's Defence Services

Defence Policy

India's defence policy promotes regional peace through negotiation and cooperation while ensuring defence readiness with modern weapons to counter aggression.

Armed Forces Structure

  • The supreme command of India's Armed Forces is vested in the President.
  • National defence decisions are made by the Cabinet, with the Defence Minister accountable to Parliament.
  • The Ministry of Defence oversees the operational and administrative control, while the Army, Navy, and Air Force each have separate headquarters.
  • The Chief of Defence Staff (CDS) oversees all three services. The first CDS was General Bipin Rawat.

Indian Army

The Indian Army is the largest branch of the Armed Forces, tasked with national security, defending against external threats, and maintaining internal peace.

The President is the de-jure Commander-in-Chief; the Minister of Defence holds de-facto leadership.

Field Marshal is the highest ceremonial rank (two holders till date).

Important Operations

  • Operation Vijay (Kargil)
  • Operation Meghdoot (Siachen)
  • Operation Cactus (Maldives intervention)

Regiments in Order of Seniority

  1. Brigade of the Guards
  2. Madras Regiment
  3. Rajput Regiment
  4. Maratha Light Infantry

Basic Field Formations

  • Divisions: Composed of 15,000 combat troops, commanded by Major General.
  • Brigades: Composed of around 3,000 combat troops, headed by Brigadier.
  • Battalions: Main fighting unit, with over 900 personnel, commanded by Colonel.
  • Platoon: Consists of 32 troops, led by a Captain or Lieutenant.
  • Section: Smallest unit with 10 personnel, commanded by Havildar or Sergeant.

Non-Commissioned and Junior Commissioned Ranks

  • Army: Subedar Major, Havildar, Jawan (lowest rank).

Indian Navy

The Indian Navy traces its roots to the East India Company’s Marine and the Royal Indian Navy.

The President is the Commander-in-Chief, with the Chief of Naval Staff (CNS) as the head.

The primary mission is to secure maritime borders and project India’s influence in the Indian Ocean Region.

Fleet Composition

  • 137 warships, 235 aircraft, 1 aircraft carrier, and 14 frigates.

Training Establishments

  • Naval Academy in Goa
  • INS Chilka (basic sailor training)
  • INS Valsure (specialized training for electrical officers)

Indian Air Force

The Indian Air Force (IAF), established in 1932, secures Indian airspace and engages in aerial warfare.

Operational Structure

  • Supreme Commander: The President.
  • Chief of Air Staff (CAS) is the highest position.
  • 5 operational commands (Central, Eastern, Southern, Western, South Western).
  • 2 functional commands: Training Command and Maintenance Command.

Training Centres

  • Air Force Academy (Hyderabad)
  • Training Command (Bangalore)
  • Operational Training at various schools (Bidar, Hakimpet)

National Cadet Corps (NCC)

The NCC is a youth wing of the Indian Armed Forces aimed at developing leadership and discipline among young cadets.

Structure

  • Divided into three branches: Army, Navy, and Air Force.
  • Conducts regular training camps, exercises, and leadership programs.

Key Notes

Structure and Ranks of the Indian Air Force (IAF)

  • Squadrons and Wings: Squadrons are field units typically commanded by a Group Captain. Flights are subdivisions of squadrons, commanded by a Squadron Leader.
  • Service Branches: The IAF has multiple branches including Flying, Technical, and Ground branches, among others.
